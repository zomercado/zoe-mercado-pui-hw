Who Is Behind This?

I'm Zoe Mercado, the creator of Creole Connections!

Growing up with exposure to Caribbean Spanish and English-based Creoles, I've been intrigued by the connections of many of our words to African languages. Through my journey in higher education, I learned more about the pan-African ideology, which resonated with my Afro-Caribbean identity.

While we have developed our own unique culture as an expression of survival and resilience in the aftermath of the trans-Atlantic slave trade, it is essential to recognize the profound influence of African languages on our linguistic heritage. Embracing this connection not only deepens our understanding of where we come from but also reinforces the cultural bonds between the Caribbean and the African continent—reminding us that we are far more alike than different.

What is Creole Connections?

At the heart of this project is a visually engaging, interactive map that allows users to explore Caribbean Creole dialects (e.g., Haitian Creole, Jamaican Patois) and their connections to African languages (e.g., Yoruba, Twi).

Users can click on different regions to discover phrases, learn their African origins, and understand their cultural significance.

Project Motivation

This website is my final project for the Programming Usable Interfaces course in the Master of Human-Computer Interaction program at Carnegie Mellon University.

The motivation behind Creole Connections is to create an educational platform that showcases the Caribbean as a culturally rich and diverse region with strong ties to the African continent.

This resource is designed for anyone curious about the intersection of language and culture, as well as those with ties to the region who want to deepen their understanding of the linguistic connections to Africa.

Future Plans

One limitation of the site is that it is currently based in English, with all phrases and translations reverting to English. This excludes native speakers from many Caribbean countries, especially since Haitian Creole and Spanish are the two most widely spoken languages in the region. I plan to improve the project by adding a language toggle that will allow users to switch between Spanish, French, Haitian Creole, and Papiamento.

Additionally, I aim to integrate more interactive features, such as gamified exercises for practicing phrases and an online community forum where users can share cultural insights.

Collaborating with historians and linguistic researchers from African universities and Caribbean universities, such as UWI, will help ensure the content remains accurate and up-to-date.
